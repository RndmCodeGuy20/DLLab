1. Introduction

1.1. What is PCA?

PCA also known as Principal Component Analysis is a statistical technique used to reduce the dimensionality of a data using linear transformations of the eigen vectors of the covariance matrix of the data. It is a very popular technique used in data science and machine learning. It is used to reduce the dimensionality of the data by projecting it onto a lower dimensional subspace while retaining most of the information. It is also used to visualize high dimensional data.

1.2 Description of the Image

The above image contains data that is represented as the linear combination of [1, 0] and [0, 1], which represent the xxx and yyy axes respectively. Therefore, xxx and yyy axes ∈R2\in \mathbb{R}^2∈R2 can be considered as the initial basis of the data as all the data points can be represented as the linear combination of these two basis vectors.

The Principal Component of this data allows it to be represented in R1\mathbb{R}^1R1, from two components to a single component. BAM!!

1.3 Why PCA?

We represent data using fewer dimensions because :

  1. It reduces the computational complexity of the algorithm.
  2. Data is easier to visualize in lower dimensions.
  3. Data has high variance along new dimensions.
  4. Dimensions are orthogonal and linearly independent to each other.

Mathematical Description

Let p1,p2,p3...pnp_1, p_2, p_3 ... p_np1​,p2​,p3​...pn​ be a set of orthonormal vectors who are also linearly independent and PPP be a matrix whose columns are the vectors p1,p2,p3...pnp_1, p_2, p_3 ... p_np1​,p2​,p3​...pn​. Now, let us see why PPP is called the Principal Component Matrix...?

P=[p1p2p3...pn]P = \begin{bmatrix} p_1 & p_2 & p_3 & ... & p_n \end{bmatrix} P=[p1​​p2​​p3​​...​pn​​]

and,

pn=[pn1pn2pn3...pnn]p_n = \begin{bmatrix} p_{n1} \\ p_{n2} \\ p_{n3} \\ ... \\ p_{nn} \end{bmatrix} pn​=​pn1​pn2​pn3​...pnn​​​

Therefore, PPP is a n×nn \times nn×n matrix. We can also represent PPP as

P=[p1p2p3...pn]=[p11p21p31...pn1p12p22p32...pn2p13p23p33...pn3...............p1np2np3n...pnn]n×nP = \begin{bmatrix} p_1 & p_2 & p_3 & ... & p_n \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} p_{11} & p_{21} & p_{31} & ... & p_{n1} \\ p_{12} & p_{22} & p_{32} & ... & p_{n2} \\ p_{13} & p_{23} & p_{33} & ... & p_{n3} \\ ... & ... & ... & ... & ... \\ p_{1n} & p_{2n} & p_{3n} & ... & p_{nn} \end{bmatrix}_{n\times n} P=[p1​​p2​​p3​​...​pn​​]=​p11​p12​p13​...p1n​​p21​p22​p23​...p2n​​p31​p32​p33​...p3n​​...............​pn1​pn2​pn3​...pnn​​​n×n​

and, let x1,x2,x3...xmx_1, x_2, x_3 ... x_mx1​,x2​,x3​...xm​ be a set of mmm data points and XXX be the matrix such that each column of XXX is a data point xix_ixi​.

X=[x1x2x3...xm]X = \begin{bmatrix} x_1 \\ x_2 \\ x_3 \\ ... \\ x_m \end{bmatrix} X=​x1​x2​x3​...xm​​​

and,

xi=[xi1xi2xi3...xin]x_i = \begin{bmatrix} x_{i1} & x_{i2} & x_{i3} & ... & x_{in} \end{bmatrix} xi​=[xi1​​xi2​​xi3​​...​xin​​]

Therefore, XXX is a n×mn \times mn×m matrix. We can also represent XXX as

Now, we can represent the data points xix_ixi​ as the linear combination of the basis vectors pjp_jpj​ as

xi=∑j=1nαijpjx_i = \sum_{j=1}^{n} \alpha_{ij} p_j xi​=j=1∑n​αij​pj​

where, αij\alpha_{ij}αij​ is the coefficient of the basis vector pjp_jpj​ in the data point xix_ixi​, and since pjp_jpj​ is orthonormal, αij\alpha_{ij}αij​ is the projection of xix_ixi​ onto pjp_jpj​. Therefore, we can represent αij\alpha_{ij}αij​ as pjxiTp_j x_i^Tpj​xiT​.
